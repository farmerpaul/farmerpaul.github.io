Grain Products are important sources of energy, B vitamins (e.g., thiamin, riboflavin, niacin and folate), minerals (e.g., iron, zinc and magnesium) and fibre.

Offer whole grains to your child at least half the time.

  • Whole grains are excellent sources of fibre and nutrients.
  • Use the ingredient list on product food labels and look for words such as whole or whole grain followed by the name of the grain, such as whole grain wheat.

  • Serve whole grain crackers instead of cookies

  • For example, limit baked goods, cookies, doughnuts, croissants, and sugar-coated cereals.
  • Keep these foods for special occasions.
  • Use the Nutrition Facts table on product labels to help you compare and choose foods that are lower in fat, sugar and salt.

Question #2 My child usually has milk products:
Open Answer
You said: 2 times a day

  • You said your toddler has milk products 2 or fewer times a day.
  • It is recommended that toddlers 24 - 36 months get 2 Canadas Food Guide Servings of Milk and Alternatives each day.
  • Having Milk and Alternatives at least 3 to 4 times a day will help your child receive all the key nutrients from this food group.

Canadas Food Guide Recommendations

Food Group Number Of Servings What is One
Food Guide Serving?
24-36 months of age
Milk & Alternatives 2
  • 250 mL (1 cup) of milk or fortified soy beverage
  • 175 g ( cup) of yogurt
  • 50 g (1 oz) of hard cheese
  • 175 mL ( cup) of kefir
  • 125 mL ( cup) of canned milk (evaporated)
Offer 500 mL (2 cups) of skim, 1% or 2% milk each day
  • It is important that your child drink 2 cups of skim, 1% or 2% cows milk or fortified soy beverage every day. This will help your child get enough vitamin D to be healthy.
  • Offer toddlers 18-24 months of age homo (3.25%) milk.
  • After 24 months of age, offer lower fat milk options such skim, 1% or 2%.
  • Be sure to count the milk that is added to cereals, cream soups, and to puddings made with milk.
  • Yogurt and cheese are milk products that provide calcium but may not have added vitamin D. Check the food label. Look for the word fortified to see if vitamin D has been added.
  • Offer your child only milk that has been pasteurized. Unpasteurized or raw milk can cause your child to become seriously ill.

See Learn More for ideas on how to add more Milk and Alternatives to meals and snacks

Learn More

Milk and Alternatives are important sources of nutrients (e.g., calcium vitamins A, D and B12, riboflavin, zinc, magnesium, potassium, protein and fat) for your toddler. Calcium is important for building strong bones and teeth and helps muscles such as the heart work well. Vitamin D helps the body use calcium.

Health Canada recommends to continue to breastfeed to age 2 years and beyond. Toddlers who are breastfeeding and are not drinking any cows milk or fortified soy beverages may need a vitamin D supplement. Speak to your childs doctor or a registered dietitian to make sure your toddler is receiving enough vitamin D.

If your child does not drink enough milk:

  • Offer your child small amounts but dont force him/her to drink milk. If a lot of attention is put on drinking milk, your child might not accept it.
  • Use milk instead of water in your recipes. Try adding milk to cream soups, mashed potatoes, hot cereal or pureed vegetables.
  • Be a role model and drink milk yourself. Parents have the greatest influence on how their children eat. If you show young children how great milk is to drink, they may follow your actions.
  • Try serving milk at different temperatures. Warm it up or put an ice cube in it.

If your child drinks too much milk:

  • Milk might fill him/her up and leave less room for healthy meals and snacks.
  • Offer milk only at snack or meal times and serve water throughout the day if your child is thirsty.

If your child cannot drink cow's milk and is older than 24 months:

  • Offer at least 2 cups of fortified soy beverage every day.
  • When soy beverages are fortified, your child will get enough vitamin D and calcium.
  • Rice, potato, almond, coconut, and hemp products are lower in protein than cow's milk and soy beverages and are not part of Canadas Food Guide as Milk Alternatives.
  • If your child is under 24 months, offer a soy follow-up formula.
  • For allergies, intolerances, and vegetarian choices, see your childs doctor or speak with a registered dietitian.

If your child does not get enough Milk and Alternatives, he/she may need a calcium and vitamin D supplement. Discuss any concerns you have with your childs doctor or a registered dietitian.

Your Resources can help you find a registered dietitian.

Learn More

Meat and alternatives are important sources of protein, iron and other nutrients such as zinc, magnesium and B vitamins.

Protein helps build and repair muscle, tissue, skin, hair and nails. Your child needs protein for healthy growth and development.

Iron helps your child to grow and learn. Important sources of iron include liver, beans, lentils, iron-fortified cereals, and pumpkin seeds.

Contact your doctor or registered dietitian if you think your child is not getting enough iron in his/her diet.

Offer 2 Food Guide servings of fish every week. Some fish, such as char, herring, mackerel, rainbow trout, salmon and sardines, is an excellent source of omega-3 fats. Omega-3 fats are important for healthy brain development.

Mercury can be found in some fish and is harmful to your childs developing brain.

  • Choose fish species that are low in mercury such as cod, haddock, char, trout, tilapia, mackerel, salmon, canned light tuna, pollock and shrimp.
  • Avoid fish species that are high in mercury such as marlin, sea bass, swordfish, fresh or frozen tuna (including albacore tuna), and pickerel.

If your child does not like to eat meat OR is a vegetarian:

  • Your child might not like the taste or texture of meat or may have problems chewing. Try cutting meat up into small pieces and offering it in soups, stews or sauce.
  • Offer other sources of protein such as eggs, fish, cheese, peanut butter, fortified soy foods, beans or lentils.
  • Offer iron-fortified breads and cereals, dark leafy vegetables, eggs, beans and lentils to make sure your child is getting enough iron.
  • Talk to your doctor or a registered dietitian if you feel your child does not eat enough. Meat or Alternatives. He/she may not be getting the nutrients his/her body needs to grow.

Question #7 I have difficulty buying food I want to feed my child because food is expensive:
Open Answer
You said: Sometimes

  • You said you have difficulty buying food to feed your child because food is expensive.
  • Not everyone can afford to eat according to Canada’s Food Guide all the time.

How to offer nutritious food without spending a lot of money

  • Plan your meals for the week and make a list before you go grocery shopping. Buying only what you need will save money.
  • Avoid convenience foods. For example, pre-cut vegetables and fruit, and bags of prepared salad often cost more than preparing them yourself.
  • Limit buying pre-prepared or frozen meals which are often high in sodium and fat. Making your own meals will cost less and be better for your child.
  • Buy no-name brands because they are usually less expensive than brand name products.
  • Buy products that you will use when they are on sale.

Call your local public health department or community health centre for information about free or low cost food programs that might be available in your community.

For more great tips see Learn More

Learn More

Follow these cost-saving tips to save money

Tips to use before you shop

  • Find out about food programs, community gardens or community kitchens in your community.
  • Call your public health department or community health centre and speak to a nurse or dietitian about ways to help you connect in the community. There may be a ride service to get to and from the grocery store. There may be a babysitting co-operative to care for your children while you get groceries.
  • Use weekly grocery store flyers and coupons and plan your weekly menus around what is on sale.
  • Buying in bulk can lower costs.
  • Plan your meals before you shop. Planning will help you buy only what you need and can save money. Bring your grocery list and buy only what is on the list.
  • Use the food you have before shopping for more.
  • Check your cupboards and plan meals using food items you have on hand.
  • Plan to make meals out of leftovers.
  • Have a meal or snack before you shop. Shopping when you are hungry can cause you to buy food you do not need.

Tips to use while you shop

  • Avoid buying too much of a food that will go bad before you can eat it, especially fresh vegetables and fruit.
  • Check the expiry dates.
  • Compare similar products to see whether a large or small quantity is a better buy. For example, when buying meat compare how much it costs per unit, per pound or per kilogram. The unit price can be found in small print under the main price.

Avoid these products because they cost more:

  • Pre-packaged or frozen meals. They save you time but cost more and often have more sodium (salt) than home-prepared meals.
  • Pre-prepared foods. Buy vegetables and cut them yourself and grate your own cheese.

Vegetables and Fruit

  • Grow your own vegetables or shop at a farmers market. This can be cheaper than the grocery store.
  • Buy fresh vegetables and fruit when they are in season. They are cheapest when they are in season.
  • Avoid buying more fresh vegetables and fruit than you need. If they go bad before you eat them you have wasted your money.
  • Buy canned or frozen vegetables and fruit when they are not in season. Canned and frozen products are just as nutritious as fresh products. If you buy canned fruit, pick a product that is packed in water or fruit juice instead of syrup; syrup is high in sugar. Choose canned vegetables that are lower in sodium by comparing the Nutrition Facts labels.
  • If you buy juice, buy a large container instead of individual juice boxes as juice boxes cost more. Or, buy juice from concentrate and add water.

Grains

  • Buy grains, pasta and cereal in bulk. This may be cheaper than buying small amounts.
  • Stock up on pasta and rice when they are on sale. They keep well when stored in a dry place.
  • Hot cereal such as oatmeal is cheap and very nutritious for all family members.
  • Whole grain products are more nutritious than white or refined grains. Choose 100% whole grain breads instead of white bread whenever you can.

Milk and Alternatives

  • Freeze bags of milk for up to 6 weeks before their expiry date.
  • Buy yogurt in large tubs instead of the individual packs.
  • Buy cheese in blocks and grate it yourself.

Meat and Alternatives

  • Buy meat when it is on sale and freeze it.
  • Buy plain meats instead of ones that come with sauces or batter.
  • Stock up on canned or dried beans and lentils when they are on sale. These meat alternatives cost less and have less fat than meat.
  • Try more meatless dishes using eggs, legumes and beans.
  • Buy a whole chicken instead of pieces. A whole chicken costs less per kilogram or per pound than chicken pieces.
  • Good value meat cuts are ground beef, stewing meat, shoulder, brisket, cross rib, outside, bottom or inside round.
  • Buy canned fish that is low in mercury. For example, light tuna, that is low in mercury, will say skipjack, yellowfin or tongol on the food label.

Question #10 My child drinks from a baby bottle with a nipple:
Open Answer
You said: Sometimes

  • You said your child frequently uses a baby bottle with a nipple to drink.
  • Your toddler does not need to use a bottle or "sippy cup" after 12-15 months of age.
  • The longer your child drinks out of a bottle or "sippy cup", the harder it will be to switch to a cup.
  • Juice sipped constantly from a bottle or "sippy cup" can cause tooth decay.
  • Offer juice in a regular cup and only at meal and snack times.
  • Offering your child too much juice can also cause tooth decay.
    • Limit juice to 125 - 175 mL (4 -6 oz) a day.
  • Offer water if your child is thirsty between meal and snack times.
    • A bottle or "sippy cup" offered at bedtime should only have water in it.
See Learn More for tips on breaking the bottle habit

Learn More
  • If your toddler is still using a baby bottle, it is time to be weaned to a regular cup.
    • A "sippy cup" can be used for a short time but should be soon replaced with a regular cup.
    • Switching your toddler from a bottle to a regular cup is a change and works best if done slowly.
    • It may take a few weeks.
  • A toddler may develop a habit of using a bottle or "sippy cup" as a way to soothe himself/herself.
    • The bottle is used for comfort rather than nutrition.
  • Instead, introduce a regular cup and find other ways to comfort your child such as carrying or holding him/her.

Tips for breaking a bottle habit

  • Start slowly.
  • Replace the bottle with a cup one feed at a time.
  • Start with your childs least favourite feed.
  • Be patient. Your child may demand the bottle for several weeks.
  • Give your child extra love and attention. Spend more time hugging and comforting him/her.
  • Change your feeding routine. Offer a cup at a different time or place.
  • The feed that is the most important should be the last one to go.
  • Leave the bottle at home while you are away or on trips.
  • Replace the bedtime routine of giving your child a bottle with reading him/her a book.
  • Offer only water in the bottle.
  • Have someone else offer your child a cup. He/she may accept a cup from someone else.

Dental Health

  • Toddlers need strong, healthy teeth to chew their food easily and to smile with confidence.
    • Healthy baby teeth also help to make sure that adult teeth grow in properly.
    • Once your child has teeth, he/she is more likely to experience tooth decay.
  • All beverages except water can cause tooth decay if these drinks stay in the mouth for long periods of time (i.e., sipping slowly from a bottle with a nipple or "sippy cup").
  • Tooth decay in infants and toddlers is often called Baby Bottle Tooth Decay, or Early Childhood Caries.
    • This often affects the front teeth, but may affect others as well.
  • Dental health is important.
    • The Canadian Dental Association recommends a dental exam every 6 months.

How to prevent tooth decay:

  • Introduce a regular cup as early as 6 months of age.
  • Do not offer juice or sweetened beverages in a bottle or "sippy cup".
  • Avoid having your child sip on juice, formula, cows milk, and other sweetened drinks all day or at bedtime.
  • Offer juice or sweetened beverages at meals or snack time only.
  • Offer water when your child is thirsty between meals and snack time.

  • Spending less time in front of a TV, computer, or playing video games is associated with many health benefits.

Why do I need to worry about my child watching too much TV, playing video games?

  • Children who spend too much time watching TV or playing video or computer games are more likely to be inactive and less healthy than children who spend time playing actively and playing outside.
  • Children are naturally active. Help your child develop healthy behaviours and attitudes by encouraging him/her to be physically active at least 180 minutes per day.
  • Children who are active have more energy, healthier weights, higher levels of fitness and sleep better at night compared to inactive children.

Learn More

Children are naturally active and as a parent you should support more physical activity.

Being inactive is unhealthy and can lead to:

  • Increased risk of unnecessary weight gain and obesity.
  • Increased risk of heart and bone disease and some cancers later in life.
  • Increased risk of adult onset or type 2 diabetes in overweight children.

A lot of sedentary behaviour or doing very little physical activity is unhealthy.

What is sedentary behaviour?

  • Playing video games
  • Watching TV
  • Playing on the computer and/or electronic devices
  • Sitting for long periods of time, or being restrained (e.g., in the car)
  • Cut down on the amount of time your child is sedentary.
  • It is recommended that toddlers 18-23 months have no screen time.
  • For toddlers 24-36 months, limit screen time to less than 1 hour a day. This includes watching TV, playing video games or being on the computer.
  • It is easier to have fun together when there are no other distractions.

How can my child become more physically active?

  • Encourage physical activity and/or active play every day. Spread activities throughout the day instead of all at once. A child who is physically active needs to take breaks for water and to rest. Follow your childs lead.
  • Have fun. Plan activities that your child will enjoy both inside and outside.
  • Plan family time. Explore and play together as a family. Try activities that include everyone. Examples are playing catch, hide-n-go-seek, tag, riding bikes.
  • Keep TVs and computers out of the room where your child sleeps.
  • Play with children and at their level. Children learn from watching those around them.
  • Play games that involve singing and dancing to music.
  • Use large safe play spaces. Keep it simple and equipment safe. Simple activities (i.e., walking, running, jumping, dancing, skipping) and equipment (i.e., balls, music) are cost effective and fun ways to promote physical activity.
  • Reduce travelling by car. Encourage your child to walk and move throughout the day unless the child is sleeping.

Being active can help your child:

  • Have a healthy body weight
  • Learn or develop social skills
  • Improve movement skills
  • Improve learning and attention
  • Have fun
