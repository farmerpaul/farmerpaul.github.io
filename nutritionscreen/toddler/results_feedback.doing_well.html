Vegetables and Fruit are an important part of healthy eating. They provide nutrients such as vitamins A, C, fibre and some B vitamins that keep your child healthy. Canada’s Food Guide recommends 4 Food Guide Servings of Vegetables and Fruit a day.

Canada’s Food Guide recommends eating 1 dark green vegetable each day. Dark green vegetables are good sources of folate. Folate is a B vitamin that helps keep your child’s blood healthy.

Canada’s Food Guide recommends eating 1 orange vegetable each day. Orange vegetables and fruit are good sources of vitamin A. Vitamin A helps to keep your child’s eyes and skin healthy. It also keeps his/her immune system strong.

Offer vegetables or fruit more often than juice. These choices contain more nutrients and fibre than juice. Limit juice to 125-175 mL (4-6 oz) per day.

Offer a variety of vegetables and fruit even if your child only likes one kind. By eating only one kind of vegetable or fruit, your child may be missing key nutrients.

Offer a small amount of a new vegetable or fruit at meals or snacks. Some children need to be offered a new food up to 15 times before they will try it and/or like it.

Serve vegetables and fruit in fun ways by using different colours and shapes. This will help your toddler learn that vegetables and fruit are something they can enjoy.

Frozen or canned vegetables and fruit are healthy options.

  • Choose unsweetened or fruit packed in fruit juice versus heavy syrup.
  • Choose lower sodium canned vegetables.

If your child does not like vegetables:

  • Serve vegetables when your child is hungry.
  • Limit less healthy food so your child is hungry and has room for vegetables.
  • Puree vegetables and put them in pasta sauce or soup or baking.
  • Offer your child raw or lightly cooked vegetables as overcooked vegetables can taste bitter.
  • Eat vegetables yourself. You are your child’s best role model. If your child sees you enjoying vegetables he/she might try them.

How do I increase my child’s vegetable and fruit intake?

  • Keep washed, bite-sized vegetables in the fridge so they are ready to eat.
  • Offer vegetables and fruit with other food groups to create a healthy snack or meal.
  • Involve your child in the kitchen. Your child can help to choose which vegetable or fruit he/she wants, and can help wash them.

Question #6 My child usually drinks juice or flavoured beverages:
Open Answer
You said: Once a day

  • You said your child drinks juice or flavoured beverages once a day or less. Great job!
  • You are limiting beverages that children don’t really need.
  • Offer water more often. Water is calorie free and helps your child stay healthy.
  • Limit juice to 125 - 175 mL (4 - 6 oz) a day.

Find more great information on Learn More

Learn More

Your child does not need sweetened beverages because they:

  • Contain a lot of sugar.
  • Are high in 'empty' calories and low in nutrients.
  • Can fill up his/her tummy and may take the place of healthier foods.
  • May cause tooth decay.
  • May cause picky eating.

What if my child asks for juice?

  • The best drink for thirsty children is water.
  • Serve juice only occasionally. Offer only water between meal and snack times.
  • Limit your purchases of juice or sweetened beverages. If these types of drinks are not available, your child will choose water or milk instead.
  • Be a positive role model. Children learn by watching those around them.

If you do offer juice:

  • Read the label to make sure that you are getting real juice and not sweetened/flavoured beverages. “Real” juice means looking for words such as “pure juice from concentrate”, “unsweetened” or “100% juice on the label.
  • Limit juice to 125-175 mL (4-6 oz) each day.
  • Offer vegetables and fruit more often. These are healthier choices because they contain more fibre than juice does.

My child drinks juice or sweetened beverages from a "sippy cup", baby bottle or juice box. Is this okay?

  • Encourage your toddler to drink from a regular cup instead of from a "sippy cup", baby bottle or juice box.
  • Offer beverages only at meal and snack times and only in regular cups.
  • Avoid having your child sip on juice, formula, cow’s milk, and other sweetened drinks all day and at bedtime (i.e., when lying in a crib/bed) so that your child will be hungry at meal times. Further, the sugars in these drinks may cause tooth decay.
  • Have your child sit down to drink beverages out of a regular cup instead of wandering around using a "sippy cup", bottle, or juice box.

Question #8 My child has problems chewing, swallowing, gagging or choking when eating:
Open Answer
You said: Rarely

  • You said your child rarely or never has problems chewing, swallowing, gagging or choking when eating.
  • You are doing a good job at making meal and snack times safe for you and your child.
  • Young children are learning how to feed themselves, including chewing and swallowing.
  • Gagging can be very alarming for parents, but in most cases, it is a normal part of learning how to eat.
  • Eating takes practice and as children grow and develop, they get better when they are offered a variety of foods and different textures.
  • The type, shape, texture and size of a food can increase a young child’s risk of choking. Offer your child safe foods to reduce his/her risk of choking.
See Learn More for tips on how to make food safer and guidelines for safe meal and snack times

Learn More

Young children are more likely to choke on food than adults. Always stay with your child during meal and snack times to reduce the risk of choking.

Children choke more easily than adults because they:

  • Have smaller airways that can be more easily blocked
  • Are not as good at coughing and clearing a blockage
  • Are less able to chew food and break it into small pieces
  • Are less likely to eat slowly
  • Are easily distracted while eating

If your child is under 4 years of age, avoid offering him/her foods that are:

  • Hard
  • Small and round
  • Smooth and sticky

Avoid foods such as chewing gum, hard candies, popcorn, whole raisins and other dried fruits, whole grapes or cherry tomatoes, hard fresh vegetables such as carrot and celery sticks, nuts and seeds.

Help to make foods safer by:

  • Cutting grapes (and removing seeds) and cherry tomatoes into small pieces.
  • Grating or cutting raw carrots and apples into thin strips.
  • Cooking hard vegetables and fruit to make them softer.
  • Removing the pits and seeds from fruit such as cherries and plums.
  • Slicing hot dogs lengthwise then cut into pieces.
  • Spreading nut butters very thinly on bread or crackers.

Make meal and snack times safer by doing the following:

  • Avoid offering your child food while he/she is walking, running, lying down or traveling in the car.
  • Sit with your child at a table.
  • Keep meal times calm and relaxed so your child can focus on eating.
  • Avoid distractions such as TV watching.
  • Teach your child to avoid talking, laughing or crying with food in his/her mouth.
  • Teach your child to chew food well before swallowing.
  • Discourage food games such as tossing food and catching it in the mouth.
  • Give your child enough time to eat without rushing.
  • Do not force feed your child.
  • Toddlers do not need pureed food and should be able to eat most table foods.

Question #14 My child eats meals or snacks while watching TV, or being read to, or playing with toys:
Open Answer
You said: Sometimes

  • You said your child seldom eats meals (or snacks) while watching TV. Great!
  • Children who do not eat in front of the TV are likely to eat more vegetables and fruit and less fatty foods and pop.
  • Enjoy eating together as a family. Aim to have at least one family meal everyday.
  • Turn off the TV, computer or video games while eating. It is easier to eat together as a family when there are no other distractions.
See Learn More for tips on eating together

Learn More

Families that eat together eat better! Your child learns eating habits and food preferences at an early age. Eating meals and snacks together as a family can teach your child about healthy foods. Sharing meals together is also a great opportunity to pass along family traditions and talk with children about their day. Turn off the TV and eat together as a family. Television viewing during meals can increase poor food choices.

Eating meals together means your child will likely be healthier.

Children who regularly eat meals with their family eat more vegetables and fruit and less fried foods, sweets, salty snacks and pop. They are also more likely to consume more fibre, calcium, folate, iron, and vitamins B6, B12, C and E, and less saturated fat and trans fat.

Why eat together? There are many benefits from eating together as a family, such as:

  • Eating more balanced meals and a wider variety of foods are enjoyed.
  • Learning feelings of hunger and fullness when not distracted by TV.
  • Sharing family and cultural traditions about food.
  • Feeling secure knowing when he/she will eat.
  • Learning about food and how to make healthy food choices.
  • Learning table manners and how to eat with different utensils.
  • Being given a chance to talk and listen.

Eat together as a family more often by:

  • Planning ahead to make quick and nutritious meals.
  • Making a schedule and buy the foods you need in advance.
  • Planning to eat your family meal when most of the family can be there.
  • Involving your child in meal preparation when possible. Children who participate in meal preparation are more likely to enjoy the food and eat it too.
  • Being a positive role model. Your child will try new foods if he/she sees you enjoying them. Try to make healthy choices part of your everyday meals.
  • Turning off the TV and enjoying meal times as a family. This is a great opportunity to listen to your child and learn about his/her day and help them learn healthy eating and mealtime behaviours.
  • Avoiding arguments. Have fun and enjoy this family time together.

Question #16 I am comfortable with how my child is growing:
Open Answer
You said: Yes

  • You said you are comfortable with how your child is growing. This is important. Children grow at different rates and come in many different shapes and sizes.
  • Offer healthy choices for meals and snacks and encourage your child to be active. Promoting physical activity and healthy eating habits will help your child grow at a healthy rate that is right for him/her.
  • Help your child feel good by praising his/her strengths, abilities and personality.
See Learn More for more information

Learn More

Each child is special and will grow and develop at a rate that is unique for him/her. There are many factors that influence your child’s shape, size and weight such as eating habits, physical activity and genetics. As a parent, you can promote physical activity and healthy eating habits to help your child grow at a healthy rate.

How do I know if my child is growing well?

The rate at which your child grows tells a lot about his/her health. Measuring your child’s weight and height once may not be enough to determine if he/she is growing well. It is best if regular measurements of weight and height are taken at your doctor’s office. Using a growth chart lets your child’s doctor see if your child is growing at a healthy rate.

Growth may be affected if your child is having health or nutrition problems. If you think your child is overweight or underweight, you should discuss these concerns with your child's doctor.

If you are concerned about your child’s eating habits, overall nutrition, or growth rate, you can contact a registered dietitian. A registered dietitian can help you understand the nutrition that your child needs and assess if your child is growing at a rate that is right for him/her.

Question #17 My child's weight:
Open Answer
You said: Is about the right weight

  • You said you feel that your child is about the right weight.
  • If parents and caregivers focus on positive healthy eating and physical activity habits, their children will follow growth patterns that are good for them.
  • Children naturally eat the right amount of food for their growth. Respect your child’s appetite and let him/her control the amount of healthy food he/she eats. It is your child's job to decide how much to eat. Allow your child to eat when he/she is hungry and stop when he/she is full. Respect your toddler’s natural sense of hunger and appetite and your child will eat the right amounts of food to have a healthy weight.
  • If you are concerned about your child’s weight, make an appointment with your child's doctor or registered dietitian to assess if your child is growing at a rate that is right for him/her.
See Learn More for additional information about growth in children

Learn More

Children grow at different rates and come in many different shapes and sizes. There are many factors that influence your child’s shape, size and weight such as eating habits, physical activity and genetics. Feeling comfortable with how your child is growing is important.

Help your child achieve a healthy weight:

  • As a parent, your job is to decide:
    • What healthy foods and drinks to serve.
    • When food is served.
    • Where food is served.
  • Your child’s job is to decide:
    • How much to eat and drink from the choices you offer.
    • When he/she is full. Trust your child’s ability to know when he/she is full. This is easier to do when he/she feeds him/herself.
  • Eat together as a family every day. Children who share meals and snacks with adults tend to be healthier and are less likely to be overweight. Family meals help to teach healthy habits your child will carry with him/her later in life. Try to fit in a family meal at breakfast, lunch or dinner once a day. Sharing meals and snacks as often as possible will have a positive effect.
  • Offer 2 to 3 snacks at regular times between meal times. Choose foods from at least 2 Food Groups. Some healthy snack ideas include:
    • Fruit and yogurt
    • Cheese cubes and whole grain crackers
    • Whole grain toast and orange slices
    • Whole grain pita bread and hummus
  • Limit juice or sweetened beverages to 125- 75 mL (4-6 oz) per day. Children do not need juice. Choose water instead. Water helps your child to stay healthy. Other healthy choices include 1% or 2% milk, and fruit.
  • Limit “fast foods” and sweetened beverages or juice for occasional meals or snacks.
  • Encourage at least 180 minutes of physical activity and/or active play every day. Spread activity throughout the day instead of doing it all at once.

How can my child become more physically active?

  • Have fun. Plan activities that your child will enjoy. If your child is bored he/she is more likely to quit the activity.
  • Plan family time. Explore and be active together as a family. Try activities such as playing catch, hide-and-go-seek, playing in the park, hopping and dancing.
  • Limit screen time to 1 hour or less a day, including the TV, playing video games or being on the computer. It is easier to have fun together when there are no other distractions.
  • Be an active role model. Children learn from watching those around them.

A child can be active and healthy no matter his/her body size. Encourage and support your child while he/she grows and develops.

I think my child should weigh less. What can I do?

  • Help your child feel good about himself/herself. Focus on strengths and abilities instead of weight.
  • Do not put your child on a weight loss diet. Focus on regular meals and snacks with healthy foods from the 4 Food Groups.
  • Do not use food as a reward or push your child to eat less. He/she may become preoccupied with food and overeat.
  • Enjoy meal times together as a family as often as possible.
  • Encourage active play every day as a family.
  • Encourage at least 180 minutes of physical activity and/or active play every day.

I think my child should weigh more. What can I do?

  • Offer 3 meals and 2–3 snacks per day to keep up with your child’s high energy needs. Do not let him/her have free access to food or beverages all day long, this will help your child to come to the table hungry.
  • Offer healthy food choices. For meals, aim for 3 to 4 Food Groups: Vegetables and Fruit, Milk and Alternatives, Grain Products and Meat and Alternatives. For snacks, aim for at least 2 Food Groups.
  • Allow your child to decide how much to eat from the foods you have offered at meal and snack times.
  • Enjoy meal times together as often as possible.

If you are concerned about your child’s eating habits, overall nutrition, or rate of growth you can contact a registered dietitian. He/she can help you to understand the nutrition that your child needs and assess if your child is growing at a rate that is right for him/her.
