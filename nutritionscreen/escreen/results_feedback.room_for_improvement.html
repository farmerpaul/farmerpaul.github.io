Question #5 How many pieces or servings of fruit and vegetables do you eat in a day?
Open Feedback >

Question #5

How many pieces or servings of fruit and vegetables do you eat in a day?
Fruit and vegetables can be canned, fresh, frozen, or juice.

You said: Less than two

Vegetables and fruit add colour and great taste to your meals. If you eat a couple of servings of vegetables or fruit a day, that’s a good start. Eating more vegetables gives you nutrients that will help maintain your health without too many calories.

Canada’s Food Guide recommends 7 servings a day from the vegetables and fruit group. A serving may be smaller than you think. One serving is one medium-size vegetable or fruit, or 125 mL (½ cup) sliced fruit or 100% juice (so a cup of juice is 2 servings).

Set a realistic goal to help yourself get closer to 7 servings a day. A simple way to start is by eating a little more of the vegetables or fruit you already enjoy. Have an extra piece of broccoli, a larger scoop of squash or a few more grapes. You can also:

  • Add sliced pears, canned fruit or berries over your tossed salad, pancakes, yogurt or cereal.
  • Keep a fruit salad and cut-up vegetables in your fridge for ready-to-eat snacks.
  • Use plain frozen vegetables and fruit for more convenience.

Learn More
Doesn’t a plate look more appetizing with colourful vegetables or fruit? Fruit also makes a refreshing end to any meal. With so many choices, vegetables and fruit can help keep your meals and snacks interesting.
Eat plenty of vegetables and fruit for these health benefits:
  • Keep a healthy body weight.
  • Reduce the risk of heart disease, stroke and cancer.
  • Improve bowel regularity.


Canada’s Food Guide recommends women and men have 7 Food Guide servings each day.

  • Find out more about Food Guide serving sizes. View the video links on the Resource Page.
  • Keep track of your servings during the day. If you need to eat more, write down a realistic goal and check your progress. For example, you could eat two servings of vegetables or fruit with each meal or start your meals with vegetable soup, juice or salad.
Make the most of your vegetable and fruit choices. Choose:
  • Whole vegetables and fruit more often than juice. When preparing, use little or no added fat (salad dressing, butter, margarine, cream, oil), sugar or salt.
  • At least one dark-green vegetable each day. Try bok choy, broccoli, green peas, green beans, green peppers or romaine lettuce.
  • At least one orange vegetable or fruit each day. Try carrots, sweet potato, pumpkin, squash, mango or cantaloupe.
  • Fresh or plain frozen vegetables and fruit.
  • Canned varieties with little or no added salt, sugar or fat.
When money is tight
  • Buy fresh, local foods, in season or on special, and plain frozen or canned options at other times.
  • Ask the grocer for smaller packages or share large vegetables, such as a cabbage, with a friend.
  • Many communities have delivery of boxes with locally grown produce. If the box is too large, share it with a friend.
  • The added cost of bagged salads or vegetables may be worth the convenience to you. Before eating, always wash fresh vegetables and fruit, even bagged ones.
Tasty ideas:
  • Mango smoothie. Blend on high speed: 1 chopped mango (or 1 cup other fruit), 125 mL (1/2 cup) each of milk, yogurt and ice.
  • Fruity muesli. Mix 1 cup (250 mL) each of uncooked oats and yogurt, ½ cup (125 mL) milk, 2 cups (500 mL) chopped fresh, frozen or canned fruit. Mix. Refrigerate overnight. Serve topped with nuts, seeds or raisins.
  • Tofu stir-fry. Heat chopped onions and garlic in a pan with sesame oil. Add frozen vegetables, diced tofu and prepared peanut sauce. Serve over brown rice.
  • Salad meal. Top a tossed green salad with leftover meat, canned fish, nuts or sliced cooked eggs. Add some fruit. Try apple or pear slices, berries or canned oranges.
  • Fruity salsa. Chop any ripe fruit. Add a little honey to taste. Mix. Serve with cinnamon-dusted baked wedges of tortilla bread as “chips.”
  • Try a new recipe each month. Borrow a cookbook, go to the library or look for recipes online. Build a collection of favourite vegetable and fruit recipes.
  • A sprinkle of lemon juice adds flavour to most vegetables. Try it on steamed broccoli or yellow beans.

Question #10 Is biting or chewing food difficult for you?
Open Feedback >

Question #10

Is biting or chewing food difficult for you?

You said: Sometimes

We all want to stay healthy and enjoy eating. That can be difficult if you find it painful or hard to bite or chew your food. You may eat less or avoid certain foods. That could lead to unwanted weight-loss, feeling tired and other health problems.

See your dentist about sore gums and loose teeth or a denturist for poorly fitting dentures. Your health care provider can help you find a dentist if you don’t have one or money is tight.

Eat softer foods such as pasta or stewed meats. Prepare foods so they are easier to chew. Chop, mince or add liquids such as broth to make food moist.

For good health, we need healthy teeth and gums in order to eat well. That’s why, even as we age, it’s important to keep our gums and teeth as healthy as possible.

Learn More
Eating well can help to improve the quality of our life by helping us to stay independent longer. It’s easier to eat well when you have healthy teeth and gums. If you have pain or difficulty when you bite or chew, you might eat less or avoid certain foods. Over time, your health can suffer. That’s why good dental care is important as we age, even if we don’t have our own teeth. Visit your dentist at least once a year and brush and floss your teeth daily.

Ideas to help you to avoid or manage chewing problems

See your dentist about sore gums and loose teeth or a denturist for poorly fitting dentures
  • Your dentist may need to treat an infection in your gums or in the bone under your gums.
  • Try denture adhesives for loose dentures. For very loose dentures an adjustment or a new plate may be needed.
Chop, shred, mash or puree food you find hard to chew
  • Find a texture that you can chew easily.
  • Meats and lettuce are foods many people find hard to chew. Try shredding them.
Eat moistened foods
  • Try: stews, curries made with lots of sauce, dahl (lentil soup), tomato-meat sauces and soups.
  • Add: no–sodium broth, tomato sauce, vegetable juice, applesauce, salsa or chutneys to moisten food.
  • Add milk to mashed vegetables.
  • Let dry cereal soak and soften in milk before eating.
  • Soften bread or crackers in milk or soup before eating.
Eat soft foods
  • Some food becomes softer when cooked longer such as stews, pasta or rice.
  • Grain products: congee (savoury Asian rice porridge), hot cereals, whole-grain pasta, soft rolls/tortillas, toast, couscous, brown rice, barley, bulghur.
  • Vegetables: soft-cooked (pumpkin, broccoli, eggplant, carrots, cabbage), mashed (plaintain, parsnips, cauliflower, sweet potato), soft cooked and cut up (green beans, grilled peppers).
  • Fruit: ripe fresh fruit (avocado, nectarine, papaya, pear, orange, banana, berries), all types of plain frozen fruit, canned fruit packed in juice (jackfruit, mandarin oranges, peaches).
  • Milk and milk alternatives: white milk, kefir, buttermilk, fortified soy beverage, hot chocolate, yogurt, quark (soft cheese), paneer, cottage cheese, grated or melted hard cheese.
  • Meat and alternatives: eggs, ground meats, stewed meat/fish/poultry, soft tofu, peanut or almond butter, hummus, refried beans, pigeon peas, beans, lentils/dahl, plain fresh/frozen fish, canned fish.
Tasty ideas

Breakfast
  • Strawberry-banana smoothie made with silken tofu, mini muffin with apple butter or applesauce.
  • Cooked, mashed pinto beans with a little salsa, pieces of avocado, wedges of warmed soft whole-grain tortillas, cut-up cantaloupe.
Lunch
  • Biryani (spicy Indian fried rice), raita (seasoned yogurt), soft-cooked eggplant.
  • Hot and sour soup, fortified soy beverage, canned mandarin oranges.
Dinner
  • Shredded jerk chicken, rice and peas, sweet potato pudding.
  • Shrimp fried rice. Use leftover rice, chopped frozen shrimp and frozen vegetables.

Question #12 Do you eat one or more meals a day with someone?

Question #12

Do you eat one or more meals a day with someone?

You said: Sometimes

At all ages, we tend to eat better when we eat together. Eat with others as often as you can to improve your appetite and make eating enjoyable.

If you are on your own, think about ways you could eat more often with others and pick an idea that can work for you. Talk to friends who also eat alone. Share ideas. Ask your health care provider about any group dining programs you could attend.

Learn More
For many of us eating and socializing go together. It’s nice to share a meal with a friend or family member or even someone new.

One way to keep your appetite up is to eat with others. It’s a great way to stay energized, healthy and connected with your community.

Could any of these ideas help make eating more enjoyable for you?

If you are on your own, find ways to make meals special.
  • Organize a potluck meal. Try to make it a regular event. Schedule it often, maybe weekly.
  • Eat while you listen to a radio program, read a book, enjoy music, listen to a book on tape, watch TV, or talk to a friend just before you eat.
  • Set the table nicely to celebrate you!
Find out about group dining programs
  • Ask your health care provider what’s available in your community.
  • Try recreation centres and faith communities.
  • Join a social club that meets over a meal.
Invite family over
  • You may have cooked for a family for most of your life and still enjoy it. Make it a bit easier by asking family to help out. Someone could bring a salad or dessert for example.
  • Invite a grandchild over. Teach them how to cook a favourite dish.
Go out to a restaurant
  • Treat a friend or family member or go on your own. A little walk and fresh air along with good food is enough to make anyone smile!
  • A change of scenery or trying new foods can help keep you interested in eating.
Sign up for a cooking class
  • Learn some new cooking tips, try a new ingredient or recipes, meet people with similar interests and eat what you have made together.
Have a picnic
  • In the finer weather, take your meal outdoors. Eat in the park, or on your balcony and watch the birds, kids play or dogs run about.
Make a date with a friend or family member
  • Set aside a regular time to get together. You might go to a museum and get a bite to eat or take a walk along a beach and pack a lunch. Go to new places, have fun and eat together.
  • When a friend or family member is helping you with errands, try to fit in a meal together as well. You might go grocery shopping or banking in the morning and then eat lunch together at your home.

Question #13 Which statement best describes meal preparation for you?
Open Feedback >

Question #13

Who usually prepares your meals?
Which statement best describes meal preparation for you?

You said: That you share in meal preparation but that you sometimes find cooking a chore.

You are able to cook your own meals. That’s a great way to stay independent and prepare food the way you like it.

Keep cooking interesting. Try new recipes or new ingredients, take a cooking class, or cook together with a few friends.

If you find things change and cooking becomes more difficult, get some help. Start with friends and family. Ask your health care provider about community services that can help. Healthy eating makes for a healthier and happier you!

Learn More
Eating delicious and healthy meals can be a satisfying part of anyone’s day. As we age some of us can manage to cook for ourselves and others may need some help. Either way, we need nourishing meals to stay healthy and to feel great.

Keep cooking for as long as you can. It can be a creative hobby with a practical reward – tasty meals.

If challenges with cooking come up, talk to friends about how they manage. Family and your health care provider might also have some good ideas. Be open to asking for help. It’s one way to take good care of yourself and stay independent longer.

Which of these ideas could help you?

Make cooking tasks easier
  • Sit at a table to prepare food. Sitting gives you a break from standing at the counter. A counter stool with good support works too.
  • Look for easy-to-use kitchen tools. Try rice cookers, slow cookers, blenders, jar openers, electric peelers and can openers, pot stabilizers that hold the pot still while you stir, knives, graters, cutting boards and more.
  • Start with peeled and chopped food. Buy plain frozen or canned vegetables and fruit with little or no added sugar or sodium. Your grocer might have fresh, peeled and chopped vegetables or fruit such as pineapple, melon, rutabaga, squash or stir fry vegetable mixes. Ask the butcher to cut up meats or buy ready-to-cook kabobs.
  • Make clean up easier. Use parchment paper to line baking trays. Try one skillet or one-pot recipes. Cook and serve food in the same dish – a pot, casserole dish or microwave-safe bowl.
  • Use the microwave more often. This helps when pots are hard to manage. Try oatmeal, sweet potato, squash, steamed vegetables or scrambled eggs.
  • Keep items you use often in easy reach. Store them together.
  • Cook when you have more energy. Make extra batches of foods that freeze well, such as soups and stews.
  • It’s OK to take a break from cooking. Keep a few store bought frozen meals on hand. They often have few vegetables so add some frozen vegetables or a piece of fruit. Eat out or order-in food for a change.
Keep cooking interesting
  • Organize pot lucks with friends. Make it a regular event. Even if you bring prepared food like a store bought fruit platter, that’s OK.
  • Get inspired. Borrow cook books and cooking magazines at your library or watch cooking shows on TV. Find lots of how-to videos on the Internet.
  • Try something new. Never used a crock pot or made a quiche? Give it a try. Look to ethnic inspired foods for new ideas.
  • Spend time with others who like to cook. Join a community cooking club or cooking class. Cook with a friend or family member.
