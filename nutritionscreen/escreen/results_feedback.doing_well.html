Question #1 Has your weight changed in the past 6 months?
Open Feedback >

Question #1

Has your weight changed in the past 6 months?
Have you been trying to change your weight in the past 6 months?

You said: No, my weight stayed within a few pounds AND Yes, I was trying to change my weight

Your weight has stayed within a few pounds over the last 6 months. That’s a sign of good health. Over the year your weight may go up or down by as much as 5 pounds. That’s normal. However, more than a 5-pound weight gain or loss could be a sign of health problems.   

You said you have been trying to change your weight and think you weigh less than you should.

Start by following Canada’s Food Guide. You’ll get what your body needs to stay healthy; eat more food or choose higher calorie foods from the four food groups to help you get to your goal weight. Exercise will help you to gain muscle, which is important for your overall health. Start slow and talk to your health care provider about exercises that will work for you.

Learn More
One way to live well and feel great is to keep a healthy body weight. It’s one of the best gifts you can give yourself!

A healthy weight is a sign of good health. It is a good goal as we age to keep within 5 pounds of our usual body weight. A healthy way to do that is to follow Canada’s Food Guide and be active every day. To get the full benefits, be sure to do both.

Sometimes pain or stiffness can make it difficult to prepare healthy meals or be active. If this is a problem, ask your family and health care provider for support.

Some things to know about your weight
  • Keep track of your weight. It’s a sign of good or poor health. Know if you have gained, lost or kept the same weight. If you don’t have a scale to weigh yourself, make a note if your clothes fit looser or tighter.
  • It’s OK to gain and lose a few pounds. Your weight may go up and down by 5 pounds during a year, especially during the winter.
  • Find help if you are gaining too much weight. You need to make healthy food choices most of the time and be active every day. If you gain 6 or more pounds, talk to your health care provider; try to follow Canada’s Food Guide and be active every day.
  • Weight loss through better food and activity choices is healthy. It’s a good idea to see your health care provider regularly while you are losing weight. He or she can track your progress and offer healthy advice.
  • Fast weight loss from dieting, or unwanted weight loss of 6 or more pounds, is unhealthy. That’s true even if you are overweight, because bone and muscle, even organ muscle, can be lost. Talk to your health care provider.
Make the healthiest food choices most often
  • You need fewer calories but not fewer nutrients. It’s a myth that we need fewer nutrients as we age.
  • See Canada’s Food Guide for great tips to make better choices. Get more nutrients while eating fewer calories. Shop for whole, minimally processed foods. For example, buy frozen or fresh instead of breaded fish. Shop the aisles for healthy choices that are low in fat, salt and sugar, like plain brown rice, vegetables, pasta, barley, beans, lentils and whole-grain cereals that you can flavour or season yourself.
  • Eat the right portions. Make half your plate vegetables, one quarter lean protein such as beans, skinless chicken or fish, and one quarter whole grains such as brown rice, barley or whole-grain pasta. Drink low-fat milk or a fortified soy beverage with most meals. For dessert, choose fruit most often.
  • Limit high-calorie foods. Eat fast food, sweetened baked goods, sweetened drinks, candy, chocolate, chips, etc., less often and in small amounts.
Exercise is critical for a healthy weight, independent living and more!
  • Everyone can exercise. It makes you feel terrific! If you have difficulty with standing or walking, you can do activities while seated. Many exercises can be adapted to your needs. Seniors’ recreation centres are a great place to learn how to fit exercise into your life safely and enjoyably.
  • Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate to vigorous exercise a week. More is better. Fit activity into your daily routine in sessions of 10 minutes or more. Stretch while watching TV, walk around the block in the morning and evening, go swimming with your grandchildren, or use an exercise bike for 10 minutes three times a day.
  • New to exercise? Talk to your health care provider before you start. Ask to meet with a physiotherapist or kinesiologist. He or she can design an exercise program to suit your needs and goals. For example, certain exercises can help you improve your balance and prevent falls. Check the seniors’ centre in your community for interesting activities with your peers.

Question #2

Do you skip meals?

You said: Never or rarely

Stay in the best health possible! Keep eating regularly. Enjoy three meals a day. If you are still hungry, have one or two small, healthy snacks. Eating regularly gives you the energy to enjoy all your daily activities.

Learn More
Eat regularly throughout the day to keep your energy level up and stay healthy. Eat three meals every day. If you are still hungry, have one or two small, healthy snacks.

The occasional skipped meal is not a problem. Regularly skipping meals could affect your energy level and overall health. You might not be eating enough food or getting all the nutrients you need to live well and enjoy your activities.

If you skip meals often, talk to your health care provider. Work together on solutions so that skipping meals does not lead to health problems.

There are lots of things you can do to make it easier to eat regularly. Even though you may be living on your own or with just one other person, habits such as keeping your cupboards well stocked are still important.
Give one of these ideas a try.
Think about how a friend or family member can help you.

Keep the kitchen well stocked.
  • Plan meals ahead. Use your plan to make your grocery list.
  • Stock your fridge, freezer and pantry with enough food to make meals for the week.
  • Keep frozen foods and dry goods on hand for times when you can’t get to the store. Try homemade or store-bought frozen meals, frozen fruit and vegetables, rice, pasta and canned foods (beans, lentils, fish, pasta sauce, evaporated milk).
  • When you have the energy to cook, prepare extra ingredients and freeze them. You could shred cheese, cut up vegetables, cook rice or dice cooked meats. Use them later to make easy meals.
Keep eating interesting – try a new food or recipe each month.
  • Ask friends or family for favourite recipes.
  • Borrow a cookbook from the library or search online.
  • Try a seasonal vegetable, or a cheese you don’t usually buy.
  • Get new ideas by watching a cooking show on television.
Ask for help when you need it.
  • Ask family members to prepare and freeze extra meals for you.
  • Ask a neighbour to check in with you before he or she goes shopping to see if you need anything.
Be budget-wise and ask for help if money is tight.
  • If a tight budget is getting in the way of eating well, let your health care provider know. There are resources and programs that can help.
  • Some grocery stores offer discount days for seniors. Don’t be shy about asking about that.
If you need a break from cooking, you can:
  • Pick up a deli take-out meal or frozen dinner from the grocery store.
  • Make a no-cook meal. Try a peanut butter and banana sandwich and a glass of milk, or canned sardines on toast and a glass of juice.
  • Some restaurants offer discounts for seniors. Enjoy the company of others, and take any leftover food home for lunch the next day.
  • Your local seniors’ centre may offer meals or group meal activities.
Make it social – cook with a friend or family member.
  • Cook together and freeze the extra food. Try stews, curries, spaghetti sauce, chili, casseroles, soups, or muffins.
  • Attend a cooking class to meet new people and get new ideas.
Keep the cleanup easy – cook meals in one pot or pan.
  • Use one pot to make a hearty soup with vegetables, beans and pasta.
  • Use one skillet to make a shrimp stir-fry, an omelette or biryani (tasty Indian fried rice).
  • Use the microwave oven to cook and eat out of the same bowl. Try oatmeal, scrambled eggs, baked beans or vegetables.

Do you limit or avoid certain foods?

You said: I eat most foods.

Making changes to our eating habits, even if it is to improve our health, can be challenging. You seem to be managing fine – that’s terrific! The better you can maintain your diet changes, the healthier you will be.

Find out about the resources and programs that can support your good efforts.

There are also websites with information such as recipes, shopping tips and more. Find great resources on the Resource Page.

Learn More
Following your eating plan can help you to live well, stay healthy longer and feel better. With some health conditions, such as diabetes or high blood pressure, you may need to limit or avoid certain foods. The sooner you find ways to manage those diet changes, the better off you’ll be.

Which of these ideas could help you?

Get the support of a family member or friend.

When your friends and family understand the diet changes you need to make, it can be easier for them to support you. They can go with you to doctor or dietitian appointments, or help you find information and recipes. They can shop or cook with your nutrition needs in mind. Having a “buddy” who also wants to improve his or her diet may help you stick to your goals.

Set goals for yourself.

Be specific and realistic. Small changes add up. Feel good about every positive change you make to improve your health.

Here’s a sample goal:
When I go shopping this Saturday, I’ll read labels to find out which cheeses have 20% or less milk fat (MF). I’ll write down the brand names so that no matter who shops for me, they’ll know what cheese I can eat.

Check your progress.

Feeling successful is a great motivator! Post your goal on the fridge and check off the days you meet it. If you don’t reach your goal, think about why and how you might succeed next time. Stay positive. If your goal is too hard to reach, change it or pick a different one.

Connect with a Registered Dietitian.

A Registered Dietitian can help you understand your diet or any food restrictions that go along with your condition. He or she can help you keep your food choices interesting; explain portion sizes; provide menu planning and tips for shopping and cooking; and show you how to modify favourite recipes.

In Ontario, there are several ways to connect with a Registered Dietitian:
  • Call EatRight Ontario at 1-877-510-510-2 to get free nutrition advice.
  • Check your local Community Health Centre (CHC) to see if it provides dietitian services.
  • Ask your doctor if he or she is part of a Family Health Team that provides dietitian services.
  • Search for a dietitian who provides counselling services at www.dietitians.ca/find
Find a healthy food shopping tour or cooking class.

Get tasty ideas, meet others and have fun. Ask your health care provider about programs in your neighbourhood. Check with your local seniors’ centre for programs and activities that support choosing healthy foods.

Make healthy choices when you are away from home.
  • Most restaurants will do their best to meet dietary needs. You just need to ask! You can also call ahead to find out how they can help.
  • When visiting friends and family, let them know ahead of time about your healthy eating goals. That way, they can plan a meal with your needs in mind. You could also offer to bring something you can eat and share with everyone.

    Learn More
    A good appetite is a sign of good health. With a good appetite, you’re more likely to:
    • Enjoy eating
    • Have the energy you need to get the most out of every day
    • Live independently longer
    A poor appetite or an appetite that comes and goes can result in:
    • Unwanted weight loss
    • Low energy
    • Muscle loss
    • Health problems such as osteoporosis (weak bones)

    A poor appetite is not a normal part of aging and should be discussed with your health care provider. A change in medications or more physical activity might be what you need to get your appetite back. Ask your doctor if any of your medications affect appetite.

    Here are some ideas to help get your appetite going. Which ideas would work for you?

    • Cook with friends or family. Swap recipes, cooking and food ideas.

    Examples are fluid milk, cooking with milk, milk puddings, ice cream, cheese, yogurt, and milk alternatives like fortified soy beverages.

    If you are having a couple of servings of milk and alternatives each day, that’s great. To meet the recommendation in Canada’s Food Guide, you need a total of 3 servings a day. Two of those servings should be milk or a fortified soy beverage. This will help keep your bones strong.

    Getting enough milk and milk alternatives is one way to help keep our bones strong.     You drink enough to be hydrated. That’s great! You’ll feel refreshed and stay healthier when you are well hydrated.

    Dehydration can happen easily. As we get older, we might not feel thirsty even if our body needs fluids.

    Women should drink 9 cups (2.25 L) and men should drink 12 cups (3 L) of fluids a day. Drink more when it’s hot, when you are active, or if you are eating more fibre-rich foods.

    Choose water most often. It’s a great calorie-free choice. Other good choices are milk and juice. Tea, coffee and soups count too, but not alcohol.

    Learn More
    The best way to stay well hydrated is to drink water often throughout the day. Keep a container or glass of water with you as often as possible. Try flavouring it with slices of lemon, orange, apple or cucumber.

    Dehydration happens when we lose more fluid than we take in. As we get older, dehydration can happen easily. That’s because we may not feel thirsty even if our body needs more fluids to work properly.

    Good drink choices:
    • Water is the best choice. Drink water often.
    • White and chocolate milk, buttermilk, goat’s milk, fortified soy beverage

    • Regular soft drinks/pop
    • Fruit drinks/punches, lemonade, iced tea

    Question #9

    Do you cough, choke or have pain when swallowing food OR fluids?

    You said: Never

    You do not cough, choke or have pain or trouble swallowing. That makes it easier to live well by eating healthy foods you enjoy.

    If that changes, talk to your health care provider. Anything that gets in the way of eating right will affect your overall health and should be looked into. Plus you won’t want to miss out on eating your favourite foods!

    Learn More
    As we age, problems with swallowing can become more common and could get in the way of eating right and staying healthy. If you have trouble swallowing and it lasts for a few days, talk to your health care provider. The cause may be a change in your health. The sooner you know the cause, the quicker you can get back to enjoying the foods you like to eat.

    Ideas to help your food go down more smoothly:
    • Focus on eating. Don’t talk or do other things while eating.
    • Eat slowly. Try this: Put your fork down between bites and take a drink. Sitting up while eating helps too.
    • Modify foods that are hard for you to swallow. For example, if meat and lettuce are problems, try shredding them. Ground meats are another option.
    • Take smaller bites and chew your food well. If poorly fitting dentures, sore gums or loose teeth make it hard to chew your food, see your dentist.
    • Eat moist food. Try dahl (lentil soup), tomato-meat sauce, saucy turkey “meatballs,” chili, curries, stews and various soups (chowder, goulash, borscht, pho, miso soups). Add no-sodium broth to meats or use it to thin out a curry. Try a little cheese sauce over vegetables. Salsa, applesauce and chutneys can add flavour and moisten food too.
    • Eat foods that are naturally smoother and softer. Try oatmeal, congee (savoury Asian rice porridge), pasta, couscous, barley, yogurt, kefir, grated cheese, canned fish, silken tofu, scrambled eggs, quiche, omelettes, ground meat (beef, pork, turkey or chicken), well-cooked vegetables and puréed soups.
    Tasty ideas:

    Change the texture to suit you best. How about:

    Breakfast:
    • Cream of wheat, canned peaches, hard-cooked egg, tea
    • Congee made with canned fish, canned mandarin oranges, fortified soy beverage
    • Scrambled eggs, whole-grain toast cut into “fingers,” slices of soft ripe mango, kefir (fermented yogurt drink)
    Lunch:
    • Leftovers: Dahl, saucy soft-cooked curried vegetables, brown rice, and mango lassi or smoothie
    • Pho (Vietnamese noodle soup) with beef and vegetables, kiwi segments, green tea

    • Slice of slow-cooked roast beef with broth, small scoop of barley and mushrooms, frozen vegetables, milk or fortified soy beverage
    • Add diced soft tofu, vegetables and leftover brown rice or pasta to canned vegetable soup made with milk. Enjoy slices of soft ripe pear or banana over yogurt.

    Question #11 Do you use commercial meal replacements or supplements?

    Question #11

    Do you use commercial meal replacements or supplements?
    Shakes, puddings, or energy bars

    You said: Never or rarely

    Continue to eat whole foods to get the full benefits of good nutrition. It is better to rely on whole, minimally processed foods such as vegetables, fruit, whole grain breads, milk, meats, fish and beans than meal replacements such as Boost® or Ensure®.

    Learn More
    The best way to stay well nourished is by eating whole foods that are minimally processed such as vegetables, fruit, whole grains, milk, meats, fish and beans.

    At some point you may need to supplement your food intake to make sure you are getting enough calories and protein. Your health care provider may suggest a meal replacement such as Boost® or Ensure®. They can tell you what type to buy and how much to take. At times, these types of products can be helpful additions to the healthy food you regularly eat but it’s best not to rely on them as your main source of nourishment.

    Meal replacements are expensive. There may be healthier, less costly ways to meet your needs. Do you use meal replacements for any of these reasons below? Which of these solutions can work for you?

    Overcome your poor appetite
    A poor appetite is not a normal part of aging and can lead to malnutrition.
    Discuss this with your health care provider. Ask about:
    • Medications that may affect your appetite.
    • Physical activities you can safely do to increase your appetite.
    • The need for meal replacements or vitamin or mineral supplements.
    • Having 4-5 small meals, instead of fewer larger meals.
    • Group dining programs where you can cook and or eat with others. Recreation centres and faith communities may have programs.
    Make cooking easy. Get help, if you need it
    • Get new ideas from friends, family, community cooking classes, TV shows and on the internet.
    • Ask friends and family for help cooking extra meals for you, chopping hard vegetables, shredding cheese, or opening cans.
    • Use blenders, food choppers, electrical can openers and the microwave.
    • Try convenient products: plain vegetables or fruit that is already peeled and cut up – fresh or frozen, bagged salads, or frozen entrees.
    • When you do cook, cook extra. Plan to use leftovers for fast meals.
    Get reliable advice about healthy weight-loss
    Some people use meal replacements as an approach to weight loss. Your health care provider can suggest safe, more effective and less costly ideas than using meal replacements. For many people, following Canada’s Food Guide and the Canadian Physical Activity Guidelines are great places to start. Talk to your health care provider before starting a new exercise program.

    Question #14

    Do you have any problems getting your groceries?
    Problems can be poor health or disability, limited income, lack of transportation, weather conditions, or finding someone to shop.

    You said: Never or rarely

    You find it easy to get groceries. With enough healthy food choices, it’s easier to eat well. That’s terrific! It’s a great way to stay independent longer.

    If it does become difficult to get groceries, be sure to ask friends, family and your health care provider for help. Find out about the resources in your community such as grocery delivery services that can help. If you need this help in the future, you will be prepared!

    Learn More
    One way to live independent and stay healthy is to be able to get groceries into your home, with or without the help of others.

    As we age, there can be many reasons why getting groceries becomes more difficult. You may at times find it hard to get groceries. A good place to start is to ask your friends or family how they can help. Your health care provider can also tell you about helpful community services.

    Here are some common challenges you might be facing. Which of these solutions is worth a try?

    Challenge: I have a hard time getting to the grocery store or bad weather keeps me indoors.

    Possible solutions:
    • Try an online grocery delivery service. You may only need it sometimes.
    • Ask friends, family or neighbours to take you shopping.
    • Share a taxi to the grocery store with a friend or two.
    • Ask about any community bus for seniors to go shopping.
    • Plan ahead for times when you might not be able to get groceries in. Keep items in your pantry and freezer to last you a few days.
    Challenge: Groceries are heavy or bulky.

    Possible solutions:
    • If you take a taxi, ask the driver to carry the groceries to your front door.
    • Ask family members to buy your heavy or bulky items (milk, toilet paper).
    • Enjoy getting out and shopping for groceries then have them delivered.
    Challenge: Sometimes I don’t have enough money to buy groceries.

    Possibly solutions:
    • Ask your health care provider about what community support is available. Cooking or buying clubs could be budget-friendly.
    • Find some new money saving tips. For example, get the cost savings from larger packages/containers of food (buns, milk, carrots, meats) by splitting with a friend or family member. See the Resource Page for more budget-wise shopping articles.
